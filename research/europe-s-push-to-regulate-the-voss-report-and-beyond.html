Europe's Push to Regulate: The Voss Report and Beyond

While the United States, United Kingdom, and Australia have each developed their own (quite different) regulatory postures toward litigation finance, the European Union has spent several years moving toward — but not yet reaching — a continent-wide framework. MEP Axel Voss's own-initiative report on "responsible private funding of litigation" was first drafted in 2021 and adopted by the European Parliament in September 2022 with a decisive cross-party majority, recommending an EU Directive that would impose a cap of roughly 40% on the share of damages a funder could recoup, require independent regulatory oversight of funders, and mandate disclosure of funding arrangements to courts.

The proposal has faced sustained pushback from the funding industry, which argues the Voss Report's conclusions were not grounded in adequate evidence or consultation with market participants. That pushback appears to have had an effect: rather than proceeding directly to legislation, the European Commission published a roughly 700-page mapping study in March 2025 examining how third-party litigation funding actually operates across member states, and its own findings underscored just how unregulated and fragmented the practice remains at the Union level, with wide variation from one member state to the next.

As of mid-2026, EU-wide regulation remains some way off, but the direction of travel is unmistakable: a 40% fee cap, mandatory court disclosure, and dedicated regulatory oversight are all now live proposals with real institutional backing, in a jurisdiction whose combined litigation and arbitration market is large enough that any eventual directive would meaningfully reshape how European matters are financed, regardless of the outcome in any single member state.
